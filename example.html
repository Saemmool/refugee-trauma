Nghiep Ke Lam, a refugee from Vietnam, had to become a parent of his baby brother at the age of 8. His mom had never really stayed at home—she had used to comeback home at around 2 a.m. and left in two hours. His dad, who had separated from her, also didn’t care about his two sons. Lam and his brother were taken care of by an old baby sitter who was also careless to them. Lam decided to take care of his brother by himself and started to skip his school until a year later, when his mom found out the absence. It took a long time for him to realize that it was part of the “intergenerational trauma” that the family experienced.


Lam, who is now 41, moved to the United States in 1980 with his parents. He had heard from his mom about how they moved into the country: His mom saw her parents got murdered by the communist party in Vietnam when the party took over the country. Lam’s family fled the country with boat and stranded in South China sea. They were rescued by fishermen who requested everything of value that they had, in exchange of taking them into a land. The family spent two years in a refugee camp in Hong Kong and finally landed in the United States as refugees. His mom did not deal with the trauma of her own life—witnessing her parents get murdered and had given up everything she owned. She didn’t really talk about it but she couldn’t move on.
